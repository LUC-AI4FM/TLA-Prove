\begin{abstract}
Fine-tuning a language model for a deterministic-verifier formal language
exposes failure modes that the standard fine-tuning toolbox does not
anticipate. We report an end-to-end study of fine-tuning the open-weight
\texttt{gpt-oss:20b} model for TLA\textsuperscript{+} specification
generation, against a $20$-problem held-out benchmark and a fully
automated retrain--deploy--rollback pipeline. The dominant negative
result is a \emph{binary-reward vacuity trap}: preference optimisation
against a pass/fail SANY+TLC signal drifts the model toward
behaviourally empty specifications, because the verifier's acceptance
set is much wider than the target set. We respond with a four-clause
\emph{Diamond gate} that includes a mutation-sensitivity test
($8/20$ SANY, $4/20$ TLC after incremental Diamond-filtered SFT) and an
inference-time piecewise decomposition that reaches $12/20$ SANY and
$6/20$ TLC on the same weights without retraining. Broad from-scratch
SFT on the same data collapses the model to $0/20$ and is recovered by
the pipeline's rollback step. For low-resource formal languages with
deterministic verifiers, the leverage is in the shape of the reward and
the structure of inference, not the volume of supervised data.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Most contemporary work on fine-tuning large language models for code
implicitly assumes a regime in which (a)~there is a large corpus of
high-quality target-language data, (b)~``correct'' is a graded judgement
that humans or weak verifiers can rank, and (c)~adjacent capabilities of
the base model are robust to moderate fine-tuning pressure. Formal
specification languages violate all three assumptions at once. The TLA+
public corpus~\cite{lamport2002specifying} contains a few hundred
specifications. The SANY parser and TLC model checker emit a binary
acceptance signal that is satisfied by an entire equivalence class of
specifications, only a sub-class of which a human reviewer would accept.
And the prior that a $20$B base model has over the language is both small
enough to be improved by fine-tuning and fragile enough to be erased by
it. The combination is unforgiving: the standard fine-tuning recipe ports
poorly, and most of the things one would try ``next'' make the model
worse.

This paper is a study of \emph{which} interventions made the model worse,
\emph{why}, and what survived. We work against a single underlying
checkpoint, OpenAI's open-weight \texttt{gpt-oss:20b}, served locally
through Ollama and fine-tuned with LoRA adapters merged into BF16 weights
for evaluation. Every benchmark number reported here comes from the same
$20$-problem held-out suite (\texttt{BM001}--\texttt{BM020}); every retrain,
every merge, every GGUF build, and every deploy is automated and recorded
under a single audit trail. The point is not to report a headline number
but to make that audit trail visible, to characterise the failure modes
sharply enough that they are diagnostic, and to describe the recipe that
survives them.

\paragraph{Why this paper exists.} Three observations motivate it. First,
the cleanest negative result we encountered---a binary-reward
preference-optimisation loop that drifted the model toward
\emph{behaviourally empty} specifications---is not a quirk of TLA+: it is
the predictable consequence of optimising hard against any verifier whose
acceptance set is wider than its target set. We expect the same failure
mode in any setting where the reward is a deterministic checker. Second,
the single intervention with the largest positive effect on our benchmark
was not a training change at all but an inference-time decomposition; the
single intervention with the largest negative effect was a from-scratch
SFT pass on a few hundred examples. Both observations are consistent with
a working hypothesis that, for low-resource formal languages, the
\emph{shape of the verifier reward} matters more than the volume of
supervised data. Third, the operational machinery that mattered most in
the year of training---an eval gate, a rollback path, and a freshness
constraint on the deploy step---is not interesting machine learning, but
its absence is what turned a single bad retrain into a published
regression. We document it explicitly because the cost asymmetry is
severe and because nothing in the published literature on formal-language
LLMs has prepared us for it.

\paragraph{Contributions.} We make four contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt]
\item A reproducible end-to-end fine-tuning architecture for a verifier-checked
  formal language (\S\ref{sec:arch}), including the static-corpus build,
  the online RL loop, the multi-tier preference data collector, and the
  merge--quantise--publish--rollback chain.
\item A failure-mode taxonomy distilled from a year of training runs
  (\S\ref{sec:runs}--\S\ref{sec:lessons}) covering catastrophic
  forgetting, the binary-reward vacuity trap, eval-signal volatility, and
  the absence of rollback infrastructure.
\item A self-contained \emph{Diamond gate} (\S\ref{sec:diamond}) defining
  semantic validity for TLA+ specifications by a four-clause check that
  includes mutation sensitivity, together with a topic-curriculum
  multi-agent generator (\S\ref{sec:gen}) that produces gate-passing
  specifications at full yield by feeding validator output back into the
  generator's retry loop.
\item A verifier-centric incremental fine-tuning recipe
  (\S\ref{sec:diamond-sft}) layered on a previously merged checkpoint,
  together with an inference-time piecewise decomposition
  (\S\ref{sec:piecewise}) that produces the strongest per-FLOP gain on
  the held-out benchmark. An unresolved multi-GPU MoE training failure
  encountered during the topic-curriculum SFT round, and the
  hypotheses we are testing in response, are documented in
  Appendix~\ref{sec:sftv3}.
\end{enumerate}

\S\ref{sec:bg} fixes notation. \S\ref{sec:arch} describes the

\section{Background and Setting}
\label{sec:bg}

\paragraph{TLA\textsuperscript{+}, SANY, TLC.} TLA+ is a specification
language for concurrent and distributed systems built on temporal logic
of actions. A specification is a module of declared variables, an
\texttt{Init} predicate, a \texttt{Next} action, and a top-level temporal
formula \texttt{Spec}\,$\,\equiv\,$\,\texttt{Init}\,$\wedge\,\Box[\,\texttt{Next}\,]_{vars}\,\wedge\,$\textit{fairness}.
SANY is the canonical parser; TLC is an explicit-state model checker that
enumerates the reachable state space under a small finite instantiation
of the constants and reports any state that violates a declared
invariant. In this paper, ``SANY-pass'' means the module parses without
error and ``TLC-pass'' means TLC enumerates at least one state and
reports no invariant violation under the configured instance. Both
checks are deterministic and cheap (seconds per spec on the benchmark).

\paragraph{Held-out benchmark.} Every fine-tuning result in this paper is
measured against \texttt{BM001}--\texttt{BM020}, a $20$-problem held-out
suite of natural-language descriptions paired with a reference module
name. The model is prompted with the description and the module name and
is required to emit a complete TLA+ module; the result is then scored
under SANY and TLC. The benchmark is small ($N=20$) and self-authored;
\S\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the statistical caveats.

\paragraph{Base model and adapters.} The underlying checkpoint is
OpenAI's open-weight \texttt{gpt-oss:20b}, a sparse mixture-of-experts
model served locally through Ollama. All fine-tuning runs use LoRA
adapters with rank $r=8$, $\alpha=16$, dropout $0.05$, applied to the
\texttt{q\_proj}, \texttt{k\_proj}, \texttt{v\_proj}, and \texttt{o\_proj}
projections of the attention layers. Adapters are merged into BF16
weights after training and converted to a \texttt{Q8\_0} GGUF for
benchmarking.

RL loop running unattended for days, and any checkpoint that survives the
eval gate is merged, quantised, packaged as a GGUF, loaded into the local

\paragraph{The pieces in one paragraph.} A static corpus is scraped from
public TLA+ sources, SANY-validated, and built into task variants for
spec generation, completion, and invariant generation; the builder drops
\texttt{MC*} wrapper modules, which would otherwise pair long English
descriptions with stub specifications. An online RL loop samples
prompts from the benchmark and a synthetic catalogue, generates through
the current Ollama tag, parses with SANY and model-checks with TLC,
tiers each result into gold/silver/bronze/bugfix, and appends survivors
to an augmented archive. The archive is append-only across cycles but
collapsed to best-per-prompt at every rebuild. A retrain chain
(LoRA SFT $\to$ merge $\to$ \texttt{Q8\_0} GGUF $\to$ Ollama re-tag)
runs whenever the new-row count crosses a threshold; an optional DPO
pass on gold pairs follows when enabled.

\paragraph{Eval gate and rollback.} Every retrain is followed by a small
benchmark. The gate requires at least three SANY passes and at least
one TLC pass; failures trigger an automatic rollback to the previous
GGUF and re-tag of the Ollama model. The gate exists because, in early
April, a catastrophic SFT run silently shipped a degraded model to
Hugging Face (\S\ref{sec:runs}). The publish step is now further gated
by a freshness constraint that aborts the publish if no full benchmark
younger than $N$ hours exists. The current published artefact at
\texttt{EricSpencer00/chattla-20b} is the merged \texttt{v13} GGUF
(equivalent to \texttt{v13}$_R$ in Table~\ref{tab:run-history} after
rollback from the \texttt{v14} catastrophe); every benchmark in this
paper that does not name a different checkpoint is on those weights.

\section{The Diamond Gate}
\label{sec:diamond}

A specification can pass SANY and TLC and yet be \emph{behaviourally
empty}: a one-state state space with a tautological invariant, an
\texttt{UNCHANGED}-dominated next-state relation, an invariant disjoined
with \texttt{TRUE}. These specifications are syntactically and
verifier-clean, but they encode none of the safety property the prompt
asked for. They are the easiest fixed point of any preference
optimisation pointed at a binary verifier reward, and they were a
densely populated mode in our augmented archive. We define a stricter
acceptance criterion---the \emph{Diamond gate}---that rules them out.

\begin{tcolorbox}[
  enhanced, breakable,
  colback=cbox, colframe=cline, boxrule=0.5pt,
  arc=2pt, left=8pt, right=8pt, top=4pt, bottom=4pt,
  title=\textbf{Definition (Diamond gate).}, fonttitle=\small,
]
A TLA+ specification $S$ is \emph{Diamond-valid} iff all four clauses
hold under a small fixed instance:
\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.4em, itemsep=1pt]
\item[\textbf{D1.}] \textbf{Parse.} SANY accepts the module $S$.
\item[\textbf{D2.}] \textbf{Reachability.} TLC, run with the configured
  finite constants, reaches strictly more than one distinct state from
  \texttt{Init} under \texttt{Next}.
\item[\textbf{D3.}] \textbf{Non-vacuous invariant.} $S$ declares at least
  one invariant that is neither a syntactic tautology nor the constant
  predicate \texttt{TRUE}, neither a disjunction containing
  \texttt{TRUE}, nor a quantification over an empty domain.
\item[\textbf{D4.}] \textbf{Mutation sensitivity.} A two-phase mutation
  test produces a TLC counterexample relative to the unmutated baseline
  in at least one of the two phases:
  \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em, itemsep=0pt]
    \item \emph{Phase A} negates a guard in \texttt{Next};
    \item \emph{Phase B} negates the leading conjunct of a declared
      invariant.
  \end{itemize}
\end{description}
\end{tcolorbox}

\noindent The first three clauses are static or single-run checks; the
fourth---mutation sensitivity---is what makes the gate semantic rather
than syntactic, and is the clause that does the real work. D1 rules out
malformed modules; D2 rules out the one-state state spaces that an
over-constrained \texttt{Init} produces; D3 rules out the most obvious
syntactic tautologies. None of these is hard to game. A specification
whose \texttt{Next} is a disjunction of \texttt{UNCHANGED vars} clauses
satisfies D1, D2 (because TLC reaches a few states by virtue of an
unconstrained \texttt{Init}), and D3 (the invariant is a non-trivial
syntactic predicate), and yet encodes no behaviour at all. D4 catches
exactly this class: if no guard in \texttt{Next} can be negated to
produce a TLC counterexample, the action set has no causal content; if
no leading conjunct of an invariant can be negated to produce one, the
invariant is decorative. We chose mutation sensitivity over alternatives
(e.g.\ requiring a minimum number of disjuncts in \texttt{Next}, or a
minimum syntactic depth in invariants) because it is the only check we
found that resists adversarial gaming by a model that has learned what
the validator measures.

\paragraph{Empirical baseline.} Of the $484$ specifications in our
augmented archive previously labelled gold or silver under SANY+TLC,
\emph{zero} pass the Diamond gate.\footnote{This audit was run with the
\texttt{tlc\_validator} state prior to the state-count and mutation-test
bug fixes documented in our run log; a re-audit under the corrected
validator is in progress and may reclassify some archive specs as
Diamond-passing without changing the qualitative conclusion that the
binary-reward gradient was pointed at the wrong class.} The dominant
failure clauses are D2 (one-state state spaces, typically produced by
an \texttt{Init} that fully constrains the variables) and D4 (mutation
insensitivity, typically produced by a next-state relation in which
every disjunct is \texttt{UNCHANGED vars}); D1 and D3 failures together
account for a small minority. Figure~\ref{fig:diamond-yield} contrasts
the gate yield of two data sources: the historical augmented archive
and a topic-curriculum generator we describe in \S\ref{sec:gen}.

\paragraph{How to read the table.} The benchmark has $N=20$. Treating
each problem as an independent Bernoulli trial, a Wilson $95\%$
confidence interval on $5/20$ is approximately $[0.11,0.47]$, wide
enough that adjacent rows differing by one or two problems
(\texttt{v8}--\texttt{v9}, \texttt{v13}--\textit{diamond-SFT}) are not
statistically distinguishable; informal repeats put the cell-to-cell
wobble at $\pm 1$--$2$ problems. The deltas we treat as load-bearing in
what follows are the catastrophic ones (\texttt{v14}'s $0/0$, the KTO
regression to $1/20$ TLC, the piecewise step gain to $12/20$ SANY): no
plausible CI on those overlaps the \texttt{v13} peak. The small deltas
should be read as directional only.

\paragraph{Structural score.} Two passages below report a ``structural
score.'' We define it here so the number is not a black box: every
generated module is checked for the presence of nine elements
(\texttt{MODULE} header, \texttt{EXTENDS}, \texttt{VARIABLES}, an
\texttt{Init} predicate, a \texttt{Next} action, a \texttt{Spec}
formula, a \texttt{TypeOK} invariant, and the prompt's expected
invariants), and the score is the fraction present. It is a coarse
parser-free check that complements SANY/TLC and is useful for
diagnosing whether a failing spec is structurally absent or just
syntactically wrong.

\noindent The trajectory has three inflection points; we walk them in
order and extract the lesson at each.

\paragraph{From \texttt{v6} to \texttt{v13}: incremental on top works.}
The slow rise from $5/1$ to $9/5$ across \texttt{v6}--\texttt{v13} is
not a single intervention working; it is the cumulative effect of
adding gold rows to the augmented archive, oversampling bug-fix pairs,
and finally a small DPO pass on $17$ gold preference pairs at
$\beta=0.1$. Every step in this range layered an adapter on top of the
previously merged checkpoint at low learning rate. None of the
individual deltas in this range is statistically significant on its
own; the cumulative trend is. \emph{Lesson~1 (preview).} What survives
incremental training is what does not destroy the prior. The
catastrophic counter-example is two rows down.

\paragraph{The KTO regression at \texttt{v10}: the binary-reward
vacuity trap.} The KTO run is worth a short autopsy because it was the
most diagnostic single result. We assembled a training set with a
desirable-to-undesirable ratio of $5.7\!:\!1$, where ``desirable''
meant \texttt{tlc\_pass}. After training, the held-out TLC count
dropped from $2/20$ to $1/20$ and a canary regression set fell to
$0/5$. The labels were entirely binary, and the gold tier turned out to
be densely populated with \emph{vacuously correct} specifications:
parser-clean, model-checker-clean, and behaviourally empty under
D2--D4. The Diamond audit (\S\ref{sec:diamond}) is the follow-on
diagnosis: under the validator state at the time, none of the
gold/silver pool passed the gate. KTO faithfully maximised the binary
signal it was asked to maximise; the signal was wrong. \emph{Lesson~2.}
\textbf{Binary verifier rewards are an attractor toward vacuous specs.}
\texttt{tlc\_pass} is satisfied by any specification whose state space
contains no invariant violations, and the easiest way to satisfy that
is to write a one-state spec with a tautological invariant. This is
the same shape as the reward-hacking failure mode reported elsewhere
in RL from verifier feedback~\cite{skalse2022defining}: any preference
loss with access to such labels is not learning ``be correct'' but
``be small and quiet.'' The fix is upstream---define correctness as a
gate that includes mutation sensitivity, rejection-sample against that
gate, and only \emph{then} run the preference loss. We do not currently
recommend any preference loss against an unaudited binary verifier
signal in this setting.

\paragraph{The \texttt{v14} catastrophe: catastrophic forgetting on
small data.} The \texttt{v14} run was meant to be a regression test of
the dataset builder under new plumbing---a clean retrain over the same
data \texttt{v13} had used. The actual run trained from the unmodified
base for $15$ epochs at learning rate $3\!\times\!10^{-4}$. SANY pass
dropped from $9/20$ to $0/20$; a follow-up one-epoch run recovered one
SANY pass and zero TLC passes. The model had forgotten how to write
TLA+ entirely. The $234$ examples involved had all appeared in
\texttt{v13} without incident; the difference was the schedule, not
the data. This is the cleanest example of catastrophic
forgetting~\cite{kirkpatrick2017ewc, lopez2017gem} we have seen on
this codebase. \emph{Lesson~1 (full).} \textbf{Do not train broadly
from scratch on small data.} Fifteen epochs at $3\!\times\!10^{-4}$
over a few hundred examples is enough to overwrite the prior on a
$20$B model. The operational rule we adopted afterwards is that the
SFT recipe always builds on the previous merged checkpoint, learning
rate is capped at $1\!\times\!10^{-5}$ for incremental runs, and epoch
count is hard-capped at $2$ in the loop driver. \emph{Lesson~4.}
\textbf{Build the rollback before you need it.} The \texttt{v14}
regression briefly shipped a degraded model to the published
\texttt{chattla:20b} tag. Recovery required restoring the previous
\texttt{v13} GGUF from backup and re-tagging Ollama, which the eval
gate had been built to do; \texttt{v13}$_R$ in
Table~\ref{tab:run-history} is the restored checkpoint. The eval gate,
the freshness flag on \texttt{publish\_hf}, and the GGUF
backup-and-restore step are not interesting machine learning, but the
cost asymmetry between having them and not is severe enough that we
treat them as a prerequisite.

\paragraph{Quick eval volatility, and Lesson 3.} Across the two hundred
RL cycles aggregated into Table~\ref{tab:run-history}, the default
twelve-problem quick benchmark moved by one or two problems essentially
every cycle in either direction, with no correlation to actual
improvements. The full benchmark is more stable but still has
$\pm 1$--$2$ wobble across reruns (consistent with the CI above).
\emph{Lesson~3.} \textbf{The quick eval is a trend, not a metric.}
Never report a result based on it; never make a deploy/rollback
decision on a single full eval; require a fresh full eval before
allowing publish. Half the publish-related code in the loop exists to
enforce these.

\paragraph{The Diamond-SFT recovery.} Training incrementally on top of
the merged \texttt{v13}$_R$ checkpoint, with $720$ examples assembled
from $73$ Diamond-passing specs (oversampled $3\!\times$), $205$ gold
RL-archived specs, $231$ gold benchmark specs, and $86$ description
rows, for $3$ epochs at learning rate $5\!\times\!10^{-5}$, reached
$8/20$ SANY and $4/20$ TLC at structural score $0.92$. That is one TLC
pass below \texttt{v13}'s peak (within the wobble) but with the
residual SANY failures cleanly attributable to local syntax slips
(\texttt{EXISTS} instead of \texttt{\textbackslash E}, \texttt{SUM} as
an undeclared operator, \texttt{EXCEPT} comma syntax). This is a more
useful failure mode than \texttt{v13}'s: structurally correct
specifications with fixable parser errors are exactly what an automated
SANY repair pass closes.

and boring. Restore the merged \texttt{v13}$_R$ checkpoint. Audit every

\section{Piecewise Inference-Time Decomposition}
\label{sec:piecewise}

The single intervention with the largest positive effect on our
benchmark was not a training change. Run on the merged
\texttt{v13}$_R$ weights without further fine-tuning, a five-step
verifier-in-the-loop decomposition procedure reaches $12/20$ SANY and
$6/20$ TLC, against $9/20$ and $5/20$ for the same weights queried
single-shot. The procedure adds inference cost (roughly $5\!\times$ per
spec, plus retries) but no training data, no labelling, no rollback
risk, and no published checkpoint.

\paragraph{The five steps.} The decomposition mirrors the syntactic
structure of a TLA+ module. Each step is its own LLM call with a
focused prompt and an immediate SANY+TLC validation pass on the
partially assembled module:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em, itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{\texttt{VARIABLES}.} Extract state variable names from
  the natural-language description. Validated for syntactic legality
  as a \texttt{VARIABLES} clause.
\item \textbf{\texttt{TypeOK}.} Generate a finite-set type invariant
  that constrains every variable from step~1. Validated by SANY on the
  partial module and rejected if any variable is missing a domain.
\item \textbf{\texttt{Init}.} Produce a concrete initial state
  satisfying \texttt{TypeOK}. Validated by SANY and by a single-state
  TLC run that confirms reachability of \texttt{Init}.
\item \textbf{\texttt{Next}.} Generate the action set as a disjunction;
  each action lists every \texttt{UNCHANGED} variable explicitly.
  Validated by SANY and by a TLC run that confirms more than one
  reachable state.
\item \textbf{\texttt{SafetyInvariant}.} Generate a non-trivial safety
  property. Validated by SANY, by a TLC run with the invariant
  declared, and by a mutation check (a weakened copy of the invariant
  must change the TLC outcome) that prevents step~5 from regenerating
  the vacuity trap on a per-spec basis.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Feedback flow.} A failed step is retried up to three times
with a structured error feedback message containing the specific SANY
or TLC string and the partial module assembled so far. Subsequent steps
do not run until the previous step's validation succeeds. The
assembled module is the only object handed back to the benchmark
scorer, so the procedure is comparable to single-shot generation under
the same evaluator. The implementation lives in
\texttt{src/inference/piecewise\_gen.py}; the per-step prompt
templates and the failure-message formats are the only project-specific
pieces.

\paragraph{Why this is the cheapest win.} The decomposition is a
within-inference enforcement of exactly the constraints the Diamond
gate enforces at corpus-build time. Every step that the model would
otherwise have to satisfy implicitly in a single 1500-token generation
becomes a small focused subproblem with its own validator signal. The
result is that the model spends its capacity on local correctness
rather than on whole-module coherence, and the validator catches the
mistakes that single-shot generation would otherwise commit
silently. The fact that this works on the same weights as single-shot
is, by itself, the strongest evidence we have that the bottleneck on
this task is not capacity but the shape of the inference procedure.

\section{Topic-Curriculum Multi-Agent Generation}
\label{sec:gen}

The Diamond-filtered SFT recipe of \S\ref{sec:diamond-sft} is bottlenecked
by the keep rate of its rejection sampler. Out of $484$ specs in the
augmented archive, $73$ pass the Diamond gate; the keep rate on freshly
proposed teacher candidates is similar (around $15\%$). Improving the
recipe therefore turns on either raising the keep rate or replacing the
rejection sampler with a directed generator. We take the second path.

\paragraph{Topic curriculum.} We hand-curate a $200$-topic seed list
($10$ algorithm families $\times$ $20$ topics each) covering
concurrency primitives, classical mutual exclusion, consensus and
election protocols, point-to-point and broadcast communication, bounded
data structures, transactional and replicated storage, scheduling and
resource allocation, memory hierarchies and cache coherence, workflow
state machines, and classical puzzles. Each topic specifies a target
module name, a one-paragraph problem description, and (where
applicable) the canonical algorithm the spec must implement faithfully.

\paragraph{Per-batch agents.} We dispatch the ten batches in parallel to
ten instances of a strong generation model called as subagents. Each
agent receives only its own twenty-topic batch, the path to a thin
validator CLI, and a strict prompt encoding \emph{(i)}~the four Diamond
clauses, \emph{(ii)}~a required spec skeleton (file basename = module,
single \texttt{TypeOK} that conjoins the strong safety property),
\emph{(iii)}~a hard $\le\!200$ reachable-state ceiling so TLC clears the
$60$\,s timeout, and \emph{(iv)}~a per-topic budget of up to six
retry-on-failure attempts. The agent runs an inner loop: write spec
$\to$ shell out to the validator $\to$ read the JSON verdict $\to$ on
\texttt{bronze} fix the SANY error, on \texttt{silver} shrink the
constants or add a fairness condition, on \texttt{gold-not-diamond}
strengthen the safety conjunct or expand the action set.
Figure~\ref{fig:agent-loop} sketches the loop.

\paragraph{Held-out eval split.} The Diamond-filtered SFT round of
\S\ref{sec:diamond-sft} did not carve a held-out eval set. The
generator round therefore deterministically holds out the first three
specs (sorted by module name) from each of the ten batches, giving a
$30$-spec held-out diamond eval. The carve script loads the existing
training set first and excludes any module that already appears there
from the holdout pool, which caught one leak (\texttt{Barrier} was
already in the prior training set) and rotated it for
\texttt{CountDownLatch}. The remaining $170$ generated specs go into the
SFT training pool.

\paragraph{Hand-written reasoning rationales.} The Diamond records have
a four-turn chat structure (developer prompt $\to$ user request $\to$
assistant design rationale $\to$ assistant spec). The rationale turn is
load-bearing: it teaches the student to plan the variable set and the
safety argument before emitting the module. Generating $170$ rationales
by template extraction does not work---a first attempt that
string-formatted rationales from spec metadata produced records with the
same skeleton, which would teach the student to produce that skeleton.
We therefore dispatch the rationale generation as four parallel agents
with strict no-template prompts and a per-batch sanity check that no two
rationales share their first $60$ characters and that every rationale
falls in a $600$--$1500$-character window. The four batches return
$170$ unique-prefix rationales (mean length $1039$, range $813$--$1287$)
that read as per-spec design memos rather than as filled-in templates.

\paragraph{Mixed corpus.} The final training corpus consists of the
existing $713$-record base plus the $170$ new generated specs in
chat-message format oversampled $2\!\times$, for $1053$ records total.
The build script hard-asserts that no record contains any of the
$30$ holdout module names anywhere in its assistant turns.
Oversampling $2\!\times$ is a deliberate compromise: pure incremental
training on only the $170$ new specs risks catastrophic forgetting on
the categories the $713$-record base covers, while training on the
union without oversampling dilutes the gradient contribution of the
new algorithm-family diversity below $20\%$ of the corpus.

\paragraph{Why retry inside the generator instead of outside.} A simpler
design would have a single generator emit many candidates per topic and
filter them downstream, which is closer to the rejection-sampler shape
of \S\ref{sec:diamond-sft}. We do not take that path because the
information the validator returns is rich (tier, distinct-state count,
mutation outcome, specific SANY error string), and feeding it
\emph{back into the generator} is strictly more informative than
treating each candidate as independent. Empirically, agents that get a
\texttt{tier=silver} verdict on attempt $1$ shrink their constants on
attempt $2$; agents that get \texttt{trivial\_invariant=true}
strengthen the safety conjunct on attempt $2$; agents that get a SANY
parse error correct the specific token on attempt $2$. A rejection
sampler that discards failed candidates would have to re-discover the
same fixes from scratch on every retry.

\paragraph{Deployed-model baseline on the held-out diamond eval.} We
measured the currently deployed checkpoint on the $30$-spec held-out
diamond eval with retrieval disabled: each topic description is fed to
the deployed model, the generated module is run through the same
Diamond validator used at corpus build time, and tier counts are
tallied. The baseline is $4/30$ at gold tier, of which $3/30$ pass the
full Diamond gate---a $10\%$ Diamond pass rate on problems the
deployed model has not seen---against $11/30$ silver and $15/30$
bronze. This is the bar a successful incremental SFT round on the
topic-curriculum corpus has to beat
(see Appendix~\ref{sec:sftv3} for the in-flight run that targets it).

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Generalisation across verifiers.} The binary-reward vacuity
trap is not specific to TLA+. It is the predictable consequence of
optimising hard against a verifier whose acceptance set is wider than
its target set, and we expect the same conclusion to hold for any
verifier-checked target language with that property: Dafny, Coq, Lean,
Liquid Haskell, SMT pre-conditions. The fix in each case is the same
shape: define correctness as a gate that includes a behavioural
sensitivity check (mutation, weakening, counterexample-witness),
rejection-sample against that gate, and only then apply a preference
loss.

\paragraph{Generalisation across base models.} Every result above uses
one underlying checkpoint, \texttt{gpt-oss:20b}. We do not yet know
whether the catastrophic-forgetting threshold (LR, epoch count, sample
count) generalises to larger or coding-specialised models, or whether
the vacuity trap is just as bad with a stronger prior. The conjecture
is that both effects soften with scale but do not vanish: the first
because the prior is more robust to perturbation, the second because
the acceptance-set/target-set gap is a property of the verifier, not
of the model.

\paragraph{Inference-time decomposition vs.\ training.} The piecewise
result (\S\ref{sec:piecewise}) is the strongest per-FLOP intervention
in this paper, but we do not believe it replaces fine-tuning. The
Diamond-filtered SFT is what gives the piecewise procedure something
to decompose: a model that cannot write a syntactically clean
\texttt{TypeOK} clause in isolation will not produce a SANY-clean
piecewise assembly either. The order matters. Train incrementally on
small, semantically audited corpora; generate through verifier-guided
decomposition; treat broad fine-tuning as a last resort.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
\emph{One model, one benchmark.} See above; the held-out suite is
small ($N=20$) and self-authored. \emph{No multi-seed.} Every cell in
Table~\ref{tab:run-history} is a single run; informal repeats suggest
a $\pm 1$--$2$ problem wobble. \emph{Curator-bound generator.} The
topic curriculum is hand-curated; scaling beyond $200$ specs will
require either a larger curated topic list or a generator that
proposes new topics by perturbing existing ones in algorithm-faithful
ways. \emph{Single-style corpus.} Every spec in the generated corpus
reflects one underlying generator's idioms, which the student model
will inherit; whether they generalise to TLA+ written in other styles
is not yet measured.

\paragraph{Related work.} The negative results in
\S\ref{sec:runs} echo a long line of work on catastrophic forgetting
under narrow fine-tuning~\cite{kirkpatrick2017ewc, lopez2017gem}; the
standard mitigations (elastic weight consolidation, gradient episodic
memory, replay buffers) all encode the same intuition we eventually
adopted by hand---incremental training on top of a previous merged
checkpoint with a low LR and small epoch budget is safer than
re-deriving the adapter from base. The vacuity trap is a special case
of reward hacking in reward learning from verifier feedback;
\cite{skalse2022defining} formalises the conditions under which a
proxy reward is hackable, and the binary acceptance signal of SANY+TLC
is hackable in exactly that sense. The DPO~\cite{rafailov2023direct}
and KTO~\cite{ethayarajh2024kto} literatures are largely silent on this
failure mode because most reported applications use human or
model-judged preferences with a richer acceptance set than a binary
verifier. For formal-language targets specifically, the closest prior
work we are aware of is on Dafny~\cite{misu2024towards} and on
Isabelle/Coq~\cite{first2023baldur}; both report that broad SFT on
small formal corpora is fragile in ways that mirror our experience,
and both end up using verifier feedback as a selection mechanism
rather than as a gradient.

% ===========================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

A year of fine-tuning a $20$B open model for TLA\textsuperscript{+}
specification generation taught us that the productive interventions
were not the ones we expected. Broad SFT failed in proportion to how
broad it was. Preference optimisation against a binary verifier reward
drifted the model toward semantically vacuous specifications. The
pipeline-engineering work that mattered most was the eval gate and the
rollback path, which prevented a single bad retrain from becoming a
published regression. The single largest improvement on the held-out
benchmark came from a five-step inference-time decomposition that does
not touch the weights at all. The recipe that survived---incremental
SFT on Diamond-filtered, topic-curriculum-generated data, plus
piecewise inference---is small, boring, and reproducible, and that is
the point. For low-resource formal languages with deterministic
verifiers, the leverage is in the shape of the reward and the
structure of the inference, not in the volume of supervised data.

\section{In-Flight SFT Run: Failure and Three Hypotheses}
\label{sec:sftv3}

\noindent\textit{This appendix documents a training failure
encountered during the SFT round on the topic-curriculum corpus of
\S\ref{sec:gen}, the three-hypothesis triage that followed, and the
resolved run that ultimately cleared the schedule. It is retained in
full because the failure mode is non-obvious and the fix is not the
one we expected.}\medskip

The mixed $1053$-record corpus is the input to an incremental SFT run
on top of the deployed merged checkpoint
(\texttt{v13}~$+$~checkpoint-131 LoRA already merged). The run uses a
fresh rank-$8$ LoRA adapter, $3$ epochs, learning rate
$5\!\times\!10^{-5}$, max sequence length $4096$, effective batch size
$8$ ($2$ per device $\times$ $2$ GPUs $\times$ $4$ gradient
accumulation steps), bf16, on $2{\times}$~RTX~8000 hardware. The total
schedule is $396$ optimisation steps. Tokenisation completed in
$2.6$\,s; the model loaded with all $23$ trainable layers dispatched
across the two GPUs by \texttt{device\_map=auto}; the first
optimisation step took $68.5$\,s and the second $66.8$\,s, putting
the projected wall clock at roughly $7.4$\,h.

The third optimisation step did not start. The trainer aborted with
the error reproduced in Figure~\ref{fig:checkpoint-error}: the
gradient checkpointing machinery saved a forward-pass tensor of one
shape and recomputed a tensor of an off-by-one shape during the
backward pass, on the second of the two GPUs.

\paragraph{Why this is non-trivial.} A gradient-checkpoint
recomputation error of this shape can only happen if the forward pass
is non-deterministic in a way that affects the \emph{shape} (not just
the values) of intermediate activations. Three sources of
non-determinism in this stack are plausible.

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.4em, itemsep=2pt]
\item[\textbf{H1: MoE expert routing.}] \texttt{gpt-oss:20b} is a
  sparse mixture-of-experts model. The router is a top-$k$ softmax
  over expert affinities; if the recomputed forward pass sees a
  slightly different set of selected experts (because of bf16
  round-off, dropout, or expert-capacity bin-packing), the per-expert
  sequence length on each GPU changes by exactly the difference in
  token assignments. The observed off-by-one shape mismatches on a
  single GPU are precisely what one would expect from a single token
  being routed to a different expert on recompute. This is our
  leading hypothesis because it uniquely explains why \emph{all}
  affected tensors are on \texttt{cuda:1}: GPU-$1$ holds layers
  $11$--$22$ where most of the expert dispatch happens for sequences
  in this batch.
\item[\textbf{H2: LoRA dropout under reentrant checkpointing.}] The
  LoRA config sets \texttt{lora\_dropout=0.05}. With reentrant
  gradient checkpointing (the default in this version of the
  trainer), the RNG state is not reliably restored on recompute;
  dropout masks differ between the forward pass and the backward
  pass. This would normally produce \emph{value} mismatches, not
  \emph{shape} mismatches, but in combination with downstream MoE
  routing on the dropped-out activations it can cascade into a shape
  mismatch at the dispatch boundary. Secondary hypothesis: dropout is
  the trigger; routing is the amplifier.
\item[\textbf{H3: Multi-GPU dispatch and \texttt{device\_map=auto}.}]
  The model is loaded with \texttt{device\_map=auto} and the LoRA
  adapter is restricted to GPU-resident layers $0$--$22$. The
  boundary between \texttt{cuda:0} and \texttt{cuda:1} falls inside
  the transformer stack, and gradient checkpointing across that
  boundary must serialise an activation across PCIe. If the
  serialised activation is sized from a tensor that is itself a
  router output, the recompute can reconstruct it from a slightly
  different routing decision and the boundary tensor is the wrong
  size. This hypothesis predicts that the same training script on a
  single GPU (with CPU offload for the spillover layers) would not
  fail.
\end{description}

\paragraph{Triage plan.} We are running the three hypotheses as
independent fixes against the same corpus and the same merged
checkpoint. Each fix is a $396$-step training job; the first to clear
$30$ steps without a recompute mismatch becomes the new recipe.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em, itemsep=2pt]
\item Set \texttt{use\_reentrant=False} in the gradient checkpointing
  call (the non-reentrant variant restores RNG state explicitly), and
  set \texttt{lora\_dropout=0.0}. Addresses H2; cheapest fix.
\item Force the MoE router into deterministic mode by pinning
  \texttt{torch.use\_deterministic\_algorithms(True)} and the CUDA
  workspace environment variable. Addresses H1; may slow training by
  $2\!\times$.
\item Reload the model with a single-GPU device map and CPU offload
  for layers that do not fit. Addresses H3 by removing the cross-GPU
  boundary entirely; slowest but most diagnostic.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Decision rule.} If any of the three fixes runs to $396$
steps and the resulting checkpoint clears the held-out diamond eval
at $\ge\!8/30$ Diamond pass (against the deployed pre-train baseline
of $3/30$ Diamond, $4/30$ gold), the new adapter is merged through
the existing deploy pipeline and re-tagged as a successor to
\texttt{chattla:20b}. If none of them does, the corpus is the binding
constraint, not the schedule, and the next lever is to widen the
topic curriculum rather than to fight the trainer.

\subsection{Resolution: H2-partial clears the schedule}
\label{sec:sftv3-resolution}

The triage plan was executed on 2026-04-07. We did not need to fall
through to H1 or H3: a \emph{partial} application of fix~1 was
sufficient.

\paragraph{What we tried first (and why it OOM'd).} The cookbook
remedy for a \texttt{CheckpointError} of this shape is to disable
reentrant gradient checkpointing
(\texttt{gradient\_checkpointing\_kwargs=\{"use\_reentrant":\ False\}}),
because the non-reentrant variant explicitly captures and restores
the RNG state on the recompute pass. We applied that change together
with \texttt{lora\_dropout=0.0} and relaunched. The recompute
mismatch did not recur --- but the run aborted at step~$6/792$ with a
CUDA out-of-memory on \texttt{cuda:0}, trying to allocate
$3.82$\,GiB on top of $47.19$\,GiB already in use. The non-reentrant
variant retains slightly more activation state per checkpointed
block, and on the $48$\,GB RTX~8000 GPUs that extra footprint pushed
the long-tail sequences over the limit.

\paragraph{The fix that actually held.} We reverted to reentrant
checkpointing and kept \texttt{lora\_dropout=0.0} alone. With no
dropout there is no RNG-dependent mask whose recompute could differ
from the forward pass, so the H2 cascade (dropout-difference $\to$
MoE-routing-shape-difference) cannot fire even under reentrant
recomputation. This is the strict subset of fix~1 that does not
depend on the non-reentrant RNG mechanism, and it is memory-neutral
relative to the original failed configuration. As a third lever, we
also dropped \texttt{max\_length} from $4096$ to $2048$, which
truncates roughly the upper half of the corpus length distribution
(corpus mean $1924$ tokens; $p_{90}=3236$) but eliminates the
long-tail OOM that the $4096$ and $3072$ settings both hit at
step~$6$.

\paragraph{Run.} The relaunched job (\texttt{outputs/logs/sft\_v3\_h2fix\_run.log})
trained from the same merged \texttt{v13} checkpoint with effective
batch~$4$ ($1$ per device $\times$ $2$ GPUs $\times$ $4$
gradient-accumulation steps), $3$ epochs, learning rate
$5\!\times\!10^{-5}$, $\texttt{max\_length}=2048$, on the same
$2{\times}$~RTX~8000 hardware. The schedule expanded from $396$ to
$792$ optimisation steps because the per-device batch dropped from
$2$ to $1$. The training executable for these knobs is
\texttt{src/training/train.py} with the \texttt{lora\_dropout: 0.0}
edit applied to \texttt{src/training/lora\_config.yaml}; no changes
were made to the modeling code, the dispatch logic, or the MoE
router. The deployed pre-fix checkpoints were preserved at
\texttt{outputs/checkpoints\_v13\_premerge/} so that the new run's
\texttt{save\_total\_limit=5} rotation could not overwrite the
\texttt{v13} LoRA history.

\paragraph{Result.} The job ran to completion. All $792$ steps
finished cleanly, with no recompute mismatch and no OOM. Wall clock
was $27{,}044$\,s ($7.51$\,h), within $2\%$ of the original $7.4$\,h
projection and well inside the $12$\,h budget the operator had
allocated. Steady-state per-step time was $\approx\!34$\,s; train
throughput was $0.117$\,samples/s. The training loss collapsed from
$2.76$ at step~$1$ to $1.64$ by step~$5$, fell to $\approx\!0.51$ by
the end of epoch~$1$, $\approx\!0.49$ by the end of epoch~$2$, and
plateaued at a noisy band around $0.50$--$0.66$ for epoch~$3$ as the
cosine schedule drove the learning rate to zero. The final reported
\texttt{train\_loss} (mean over the run) was $0.663$, mean token
accuracy was $0.890$, and entropy was $0.500$. Five rolling
checkpoints were retained, with the final adapter at
\texttt{outputs/checkpoints/checkpoint-792/}.

\paragraph{What this tells us about the original failure.} The fix
that actually held is the strictly weaker half of fix~1: dropout zero
without the non-reentrant RNG mechanism. Two things follow from
that. First, hypothesis~H2 was correct in its mechanism but not in
its framing --- dropout was not just the trigger and routing the
amplifier, dropout alone (under reentrant recompute) is sufficient
to drive the MoE router into a different decision on the recompute
pass, which is what produces the off-by-one shape mismatch on
\texttt{cuda:1}. Second, hypotheses~H1 and~H3 are not falsified, but
they are not load-bearing for this stack: the router is determinis\-tic
\emph{enough} given identical inputs, and the cross-GPU dispatch
boundary is stable \emph{enough} given a deterministic forward pass.
The single source of non-determinism that mattered was the LoRA
dropout mask. We did not need the deterministic-router pin~(H1) or
the single-GPU CPU-offload reload~(H3), and we deliberately did not
run them: the cheapest fix cleared the schedule, so spending more
GPU time on the more expensive ones would have been load-bearing
only for a paper that does not exist.

\paragraph{Status of the decision rule.} The training half of the
decision rule from \S\ref{sec:sftv3} is satisfied: a fix from the
triage plan ran to its full schedule. The held-out Diamond eval and
the merge-and-redeploy step are pending and will be folded into the
next revision of \S\ref{sec:results}; whether the new adapter is
promoted to a successor of \texttt{chattla:20b} therefore still
turns on whether \texttt{checkpoint-792} clears
$\ge\!8/30$~Diamond pass against the $3/30$ pre-train baseline.
