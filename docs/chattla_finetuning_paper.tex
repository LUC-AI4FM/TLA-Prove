\begin{abstract}
Formal specification languages such as TLA\textsuperscript{+} present an
unusually sharp test for language-model fine-tuning: the public corpus is
small (a few hundred specifications), the verifier is a deterministic
parser--model-checker pair (SANY and TLC), and the cost of a subtly wrong
specification is high enough that there is little useful gradient between
``almost right'' and ``right.'' This combination invites the standard
contemporary fine-tuning toolbox---broad supervised fine-tuning, preference
optimisation against verifier rewards, distillation from larger
teachers---and resists most of it.

We report a controlled, end-to-end study of fine-tuning a single open-weight
\texttt{gpt-oss:20b} checkpoint for TLA\textsuperscript{+} specification
generation. Across more than two hundred reinforcement-learning cycles,
several supervised regimes, and an automated rebuild--deploy--rollback
pipeline, we identify three load-bearing failure modes: \emph{(i)}
catastrophic forgetting under broad fine-tuning on small corpora,
\emph{(ii)} a binary-reward vacuity trap in which preference optimisation
against a pass/fail verifier signal drifts the model toward semantically
empty specifications, and \emph{(iii)} systematic over-reading of
small-benchmark fluctuations. We then propose and evaluate a
verifier-centric recipe built around a four-clause \emph{Diamond gate} that
includes a mutation-sensitivity test, a topic-curriculum multi-agent
generator that produces gate-passing specifications at full yield, and an
incremental low-rate SFT schedule layered on a previously merged
checkpoint. The strongest end-to-end checkpoint reaches $9/20$ SANY and
$5/20$ TLC on our held-out benchmark; an inference-time decomposition layer
on the same checkpoint reaches $12/20$ and $6/20$ without modifying
weights. The strongest catastrophe collapses the same model to $0/20$ on
both metrics. We document the engineering of the pipeline, the failure
modes, the recipe that survives them, and one fresh failure---a gradient
checkpoint recomputation mismatch under multi-GPU MoE dispatch---together
with the hypotheses we are testing in response. The contribution is not a
single number; it is a reproducible audit of which interventions help,
which hurt, and what the failures imply about training models for
verifier-checked formal languages.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Most contemporary work on fine-tuning large language models for code
implicitly assumes a regime in which (a)~there is a large corpus of
high-quality target-language data, (b)~``correct'' is a graded judgement
that humans or weak verifiers can rank, and (c)~adjacent capabilities of
the base model are robust to moderate fine-tuning pressure. Formal
specification languages violate all three assumptions at once. The TLA+
public corpus~\cite{lamport2002specifying} contains a few hundred
specifications. The SANY parser and TLC model checker emit a binary
acceptance signal that is satisfied by an entire equivalence class of
specifications, only a sub-class of which a human reviewer would accept.
And the prior that a $20$B base model has over the language is both small
enough to be improved by fine-tuning and fragile enough to be erased by
it. The combination is unforgiving: the standard fine-tuning recipe ports
poorly, and most of the things one would try ``next'' make the model
worse.

This paper is a study of \emph{which} interventions made the model worse,
\emph{why}, and what survived. We work against a single underlying
checkpoint, OpenAI's open-weight \texttt{gpt-oss:20b}, served locally
through Ollama and fine-tuned with LoRA adapters merged into BF16 weights
for evaluation. Every benchmark number reported here comes from the same
$20$-problem held-out suite (\texttt{BM001}--\texttt{BM020}); every retrain,
every merge, every GGUF build, and every deploy is automated and recorded
under a single audit trail. The point is not to report a headline number
but to make that audit trail visible, to characterise the failure modes
sharply enough that they are diagnostic, and to describe the recipe that
survives them.

\paragraph{Why this paper exists.} Three observations motivate it. First,
the cleanest negative result we encountered---a binary-reward
preference-optimisation loop that drifted the model toward
\emph{behaviourally empty} specifications---is not a quirk of TLA+: it is
the predictable consequence of optimising hard against any verifier whose
acceptance set is wider than its target set. We expect the same failure
mode in any setting where the reward is a deterministic checker. Second,
the single intervention with the largest positive effect on our benchmark
was not a training change at all but an inference-time decomposition; the
single intervention with the largest negative effect was a from-scratch
SFT pass on a few hundred examples. Both observations are consistent with
a working hypothesis that, for low-resource formal languages, the
\emph{shape of the verifier reward} matters more than the volume of
supervised data. Third, the operational machinery that mattered most in
the year of training---an eval gate, a rollback path, and a freshness
constraint on the deploy step---is not interesting machine learning, but
its absence is what turned a single bad retrain into a published
regression. We document it explicitly because the cost asymmetry is
severe and because nothing in the published literature on formal-language
LLMs has prepared us for it.

\paragraph{Contributions.} We make four contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em,itemsep=2pt]
\item A reproducible end-to-end fine-tuning architecture for a verifier-checked
  formal language (\S\ref{sec:arch}), including the static-corpus build,
  the online RL loop, the multi-tier preference data collector, and the
  merge--quantise--publish--rollback chain.
\item A failure-mode taxonomy distilled from a year of training runs
  (\S\ref{sec:runs}--\S\ref{sec:lessons}) covering catastrophic
  forgetting, the binary-reward vacuity trap, eval-signal volatility, and
  the absence of rollback infrastructure.
\item A self-contained \emph{Diamond gate} (\S\ref{sec:diamond}) defining
  semantic validity for TLA+ specifications by a four-clause check that
  includes mutation sensitivity, together with a topic-curriculum
  multi-agent generator (\S\ref{sec:gen}) that produces gate-passing
  specifications at full yield by feeding validator output back into the
  generator's retry loop.
\item A verifier-centric incremental fine-tuning recipe
  (\S\ref{sec:diamond-sft}--\S\ref{sec:sftv3}) layered on a previously
  merged checkpoint, an honest accounting of its in-flight failure mode
  on multi-GPU MoE dispatch, and the hypotheses we are testing in
  response.
\end{enumerate}

\S\ref{sec:discussion} discusses generalisation and limitations and
\S\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

\section{Background and Setting}
\label{sec:bg}

\paragraph{TLA\textsuperscript{+}, SANY, TLC.} TLA+ is a specification
language for concurrent and distributed systems built on temporal logic
of actions. A specification is a module of declared variables, an
\texttt{Init} predicate, a \texttt{Next} action, and a top-level temporal
formula \texttt{Spec}\,$\,\equiv\,$\,\texttt{Init}\,$\wedge\,\Box[\,\texttt{Next}\,]_{vars}\,\wedge\,$\textit{fairness}.
SANY is the canonical parser; TLC is an explicit-state model checker that
enumerates the reachable state space under a small finite instantiation
of the constants and reports any state that violates a declared
invariant. In this paper, ``SANY-pass'' means the module parses without
error and ``TLC-pass'' means TLC enumerates at least one state and
reports no invariant violation under the configured instance. Both
checks are deterministic and cheap (seconds per spec on the benchmark).

\paragraph{Held-out benchmark.} Every fine-tuning result in this paper is
measured against \texttt{BM001}--\texttt{BM020}, a $20$-problem held-out
suite of natural-language descriptions paired with a reference module
name. The model is prompted with the description and the module name and
is required to emit a complete TLA+ module; the result is then scored
under SANY and TLC. The benchmark is small ($N=20$) and self-authored;
\S\ref{sec:discussion} discusses the statistical caveats.

\paragraph{Base model and adapters.} The underlying checkpoint is
OpenAI's open-weight \texttt{gpt-oss:20b}, a sparse mixture-of-experts
model served locally through Ollama. All fine-tuning runs use LoRA
adapters with rank $r=8$, $\alpha=16$, dropout $0.05$, applied to the
\texttt{q\_proj}, \texttt{k\_proj}, \texttt{v\_proj}, and \texttt{o\_proj}
projections of the attention layers. Adapters are merged into BF16
weights after training and converted to a \texttt{Q8\_0} GGUF for
benchmarking.

RL loop running unattended for days, and any checkpoint that survives the
eval gate is merged, quantised, packaged as a GGUF, loaded into the local
Ollama daemon, and (optionally) published to Hugging Face. Anything that
fails the eval gate is rolled back. Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} gives the
end-to-end view; the paragraphs below describe the moving parts.

\paragraph{Static corpus.} Raw TLA+ sources are scraped into
\texttt{data/raw/}, validated through SANY, and written to
\texttt{data/validated/combined.jsonl}. A dataset builder produces task
variants for specification generation, completion, and invariant
generation. Production flags drop non-parsing rows, append the
RL-harvested gold archive, mix in English-to-spec rows for description
coverage, and oversample bug-fix pairs. One subtle trap: the public
corpus is full of \texttt{MC*} wrapper modules whose only content is
\texttt{EXTENDS Real\_protocol, TLC}; training on these pairs a long
English description with a stub specification and poisons the
spec-generation task. The dataset builder drops them by family.

\paragraph{Online RL loop.} The continuous training loop drives
generation, validation, tiering, augmentation, and retrain decisions.
Each cycle runs six phases: \emph{(1)}~prompt sampling from the
benchmark, the synthetic \texttt{RL*} catalogue, and condensed
natural-language descriptions; \emph{(2)}~spec generation through Ollama
against the current \texttt{chattla:20b} tag, followed by SANY parsing
and TLC model checking; \emph{(3)}~tiering into gold, silver, bronze, and
\emph{bugfix} (silver $+$ TLC error string $\to$ gold target) classes;
\emph{(4)}~retrain when accumulated new rows cross a threshold;
\emph{(5)}~quick benchmark every cycle and full benchmark on a nightly
cadence; \emph{(6)}~difficulty cap from history so the loop does not
over-sample hard prompts while TLC is still weak.

\paragraph{Retrain chain.} A retrain is a four-step subprocess chain:
dataset builder $\to$ \texttt{train.py} (LoRA SFT in harmony chat format
with VRAM-aware sequence length) $\to$ \texttt{merge\_lora.py}
(dual-GPU with CPU fallback) $\to$ \texttt{convert\_to\_gguf.py}
(\texttt{llama.cpp} convert at \texttt{Q8\_0}, registering the artefact
locally as \texttt{chattla:20b}). When DPO is enabled,
\texttt{train\_dpo.run\_after\_sft} runs on gold pairs with $\beta=0.1$
and learning rate $5\!\times\!10^{-6}$.

\paragraph{Augmented archive.} The augmented archive
(\texttt{data/processed/augmented.jsonl}) is append-only across RL
cycles. Nothing is deleted as the benchmark moves; each rebuild collapses
augmented rows to one row per prompt id, keeping the highest-tier
survivor (gold and bug-fix dominate silver, which dominates bronze). New
gold replaces old silver for the same prompt. This is the smallest
mechanism we found for keeping the dataset monotonically improving across
cycles without manual pruning.

\paragraph{Eval gate and rollback.} Every retrain is followed by a small
benchmark (ten problems by default). The gate requires at least three
SANY passes and at least one TLC pass; failures trigger an automatic
rollback to the previous GGUF and re-tag of the Ollama model. The gate
exists because, in early April, a catastrophic SFT run silently shipped
a degraded model to Hugging Face. The publish step is now further gated
by a freshness constraint that aborts the publish if no full benchmark
younger than $N$ hours exists.

\section{The Diamond Gate}
\label{sec:diamond}

A specification can pass SANY and TLC and yet be \emph{behaviourally
empty}: a one-state state space with a tautological invariant, an
\texttt{UNCHANGED}-dominated next-state relation, an invariant disjoined
with \texttt{TRUE}. These specifications are syntactically and
verifier-clean, but they encode none of the safety property the prompt
asked for. They are the easiest fixed point of any preference
optimisation pointed at a binary verifier reward, and they were a
densely populated mode in our augmented archive. We define a stricter
acceptance criterion---the \emph{Diamond gate}---that rules them out.

\begin{tcolorbox}[
  enhanced, breakable,
  colback=cbox, colframe=cline, boxrule=0.5pt,
  arc=2pt, left=8pt, right=8pt, top=4pt, bottom=4pt,
  title=\textbf{Definition (Diamond gate).}, fonttitle=\small,
]
A TLA+ specification $S$ is \emph{Diamond-valid} iff all four clauses
hold under a small fixed instance:
\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.4em, itemsep=1pt]
\item[\textbf{D1.}] \textbf{Parse.} SANY accepts the module $S$.
\item[\textbf{D2.}] \textbf{Reachability.} TLC, run with the configured
  finite constants, reaches strictly more than one distinct state from
  \texttt{Init} under \texttt{Next}.
\item[\textbf{D3.}] \textbf{Non-vacuous invariant.} $S$ declares at least
  one invariant that is neither a syntactic tautology nor the constant
  predicate \texttt{TRUE}, neither a disjunction containing
  \texttt{TRUE}, nor a quantification over an empty domain.
\item[\textbf{D4.}] \textbf{Mutation sensitivity.} A two-phase mutation
  test produces a TLC counterexample relative to the unmutated baseline
  in at least one of the two phases:
  \begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.2em, itemsep=0pt]
    \item \emph{Phase A} negates a guard in \texttt{Next};
    \item \emph{Phase B} negates the leading conjunct of a declared
      invariant.
  \end{itemize}
\end{description}
\end{tcolorbox}

\noindent The first three clauses are static or single-run checks; the
fourth requires two additional TLC invocations on textually mutated
copies of the module and is what makes the gate semantic rather than
syntactic. Intuitively: a spec that passes D1--D3 but is mutation-blind
under D4 has either no real safety conjunct or no real action behaviour,
and is exactly the class that the binary-reward optimisation we
discuss in \S\ref{sec:lessons} drifted toward.

\paragraph{Empirical baseline.} Of the $484$ specifications in our
augmented archive previously labelled gold or silver under SANY+TLC,
\emph{zero} pass the Diamond gate. The dominant failure clauses are D2
(one-state state spaces, often produced by an \texttt{Init} that fully
constrains the variables) and D4 (mutation insensitivity, often produced
by a next-state relation in which every disjunct is \texttt{UNCHANGED
vars}). Figure~\ref{fig:diamond-yield} contrasts the gate yield of two
data sources: the historical augmented archive and a topic-curriculum
generator we describe in \S\ref{sec:gen}.

because it was the most diagnostic single result. We assembled a
training set with a desirable-to-undesirable ratio of $5.7\!:\!1$, where
``desirable'' meant \texttt{tlc\_pass}. After training, the held-out TLC
count dropped from $2/20$ to $1/20$ and a canary regression set fell to
$0/5$. The labels were entirely binary and the gold tier turned out to
contain a large fraction of \emph{vacuously correct} specifications:
parser-clean, model-checker-clean, and behaviourally empty under D2--D4.
The Diamond audit (\S\ref{sec:diamond}) is the follow-on diagnosis:
$0/484$ of the gold/silver pool passes the gate. KTO faithfully maximised
the binary signal it was asked to maximise; the signal was wrong.

\paragraph{The catastrophe.} The \texttt{v14} run was a regression test
of the dataset builder under new plumbing. The intent was a clean
retrain over the same data \texttt{v13} had used. The actual run trained
from the unmodified base for $15$ epochs at learning rate
$3\!\times\!10^{-4}$. SANY pass dropped from $9/20$ to $0/20$ and a
follow-up SFT-only pass recovered exactly one SANY pass and zero TLC
passes; the model had forgotten how to write TLA+ entirely. Recovery
required restoring the previous \texttt{v13} GGUF from backup and
re-tagging Ollama, which the eval gate had been built to do.

\paragraph{The Diamond-SFT recovery.} Training incrementally on top of
the merged \texttt{v13}$_R$ checkpoint, with $720$ examples assembled
from $73$ Diamond-passing specs (oversampled $3\!\times$), $205$ gold
RL-archived specs, $231$ gold benchmark specs, and $86$ description rows,
for $3$ epochs at learning rate $5\!\times\!10^{-5}$, reached $8/20$
SANY and $4/20$ TLC. That is one TLC pass below \texttt{v13}'s peak but
with much higher structural quality (mean $0.92$ versus $0.77$) and with
the residual SANY failures cleanly attributable to local syntax slips
(\texttt{EXISTS} instead of \texttt{\textbackslash E}, \texttt{SUM} as an
undeclared operator, \texttt{EXCEPT} comma syntax). This is a more
useful failure mode than \texttt{v13}'s: structurally correct
specifications with fixable parser errors are exactly what an automated
SANY repair pass closes.

\section{Four Lessons from the Log}
\label{sec:lessons}

\paragraph{Lesson 1: Do not train broadly from scratch on small data.}
The catastrophe is the cleanest example of catastrophic forgetting we
have ever seen on this codebase. The $234$ examples involved had all
appeared in \texttt{v13} without incident; the difference was that
\texttt{v13} added them on top of an already TLA+-fluent merged
checkpoint, while the catastrophe re-derived the adapter from the
unmodified base. Fifteen epochs at $3\!\times\!10^{-4}$ over a few
hundred examples is enough to overwrite the prior. The operational rule
we adopted afterwards is that the SFT recipe always builds on the
previous merged checkpoint, learning rate is capped at
$1\!\times\!10^{-5}$ for incremental runs, and epoch count is hard-capped
at $2$ in the loop driver.

\paragraph{Lesson 2: Binary verifier rewards are an attractor toward
vacuous specs.} Preference-learning losses are well-understood and they
behaved correctly given the labels we gave them. The labels were the
bug. \texttt{tlc\_pass} is satisfied by \emph{any} specification whose
state space contains no invariant violations, and the easiest way to
satisfy that is to write a specification with a one-state state space
and a tautological invariant. The training corpus we built up over
months of RL was densely populated with exactly that class of spec
($0/484$ Diamond-passing). Any preference-optimisation loop pointed at
this corpus is not learning ``be correct''; it is learning ``be small
and quiet.'' The fix is upstream: define correctness as a four-clause
gate that includes mutation sensitivity, harvest the corpus by
rejection sampling against that gate, and only \emph{then} run the
preference loss.

\paragraph{Lesson 3: The quick eval is a trend, not a metric.} The
default quick benchmark is twelve problems. Across two hundred RL
cycles, the quick eval moved by one or two problems essentially every
cycle, in either direction, with no correlation to actual model
improvements. The full benchmark is more stable but still has
$\pm 1$--$2$ problem wobble across reruns. The operational rule we
adopted is: never report a result based on the quick eval, never make a
deploy/rollback decision on a single full eval, and require a fresh full
eval within $N$ hours before allowing the publish step to proceed. Half
the publish-related code in the loop exists to enforce this.

\paragraph{Lesson 4: Build the rollback before you need it.} The
\texttt{v14} regression caused real damage: a degraded model was,
briefly, the published \texttt{chattla:20b} tag. The eval gate, the
freshness flag on \texttt{publish\_hf}, and the GGUF backup-and-restore
step were all written in response. None of them are interesting machine
learning; all of them are interesting MLOps. The asymmetry between the
cost of building rollback infrastructure and the cost of not having it
is severe enough that we now treat rollback as a prerequisite, not a
nice-to-have.

\section{Topic-Curriculum Multi-Agent Generation}
\label{sec:gen}

The Diamond-filtered SFT recipe of \S\ref{sec:diamond-sft} is bottlenecked
by the keep rate of its rejection sampler. Out of $484$ specs in the
augmented archive, $73$ pass the Diamond gate; the keep rate on freshly
proposed teacher candidates is similar (around $15\%$). Improving the
recipe therefore turns on either raising the keep rate or replacing the
rejection sampler with a directed generator. We take the second path.

\paragraph{Topic curriculum.} We hand-curate a $200$-topic seed list
($10$ algorithm families $\times$ $20$ topics each) covering
concurrency primitives, classical mutual exclusion, consensus and
election protocols, point-to-point and broadcast communication, bounded
data structures, transactional and replicated storage, scheduling and
resource allocation, memory hierarchies and cache coherence, workflow
state machines, and classical puzzles. Each topic specifies a target
module name, a one-paragraph problem description, and (where
applicable) the canonical algorithm the spec must implement faithfully.

\paragraph{Per-batch agents.} We dispatch the ten batches in parallel to
ten instances of a strong generation model called as subagents. Each
agent receives only its own twenty-topic batch, the path to a thin
validator CLI, and a strict prompt encoding \emph{(i)}~the four Diamond
clauses, \emph{(ii)}~a required spec skeleton (file basename = module,
single \texttt{TypeOK} that conjoins the strong safety property),
\emph{(iii)}~a hard $\le\!200$ reachable-state ceiling so TLC clears the
$60$\,s timeout, and \emph{(iv)}~a per-topic budget of up to six
retry-on-failure attempts. The agent runs an inner loop: write spec
$\to$ shell out to the validator $\to$ read the JSON verdict $\to$ on
\texttt{bronze} fix the SANY error, on \texttt{silver} shrink the
constants or add a fairness condition, on \texttt{gold-not-diamond}
strengthen the safety conjunct or expand the action set.
Figure~\ref{fig:agent-loop} sketches the loop.

\paragraph{Held-out eval split.} The Diamond-filtered SFT round of
\S\ref{sec:diamond-sft} did not carve a held-out eval set. The
generator round therefore deterministically holds out the first three
specs (sorted by module name) from each of the ten batches, giving a
$30$-spec held-out diamond eval. The carve script loads the existing
training set first and excludes any module that already appears there
from the holdout pool, which caught one leak (\texttt{Barrier} was
already in the prior training set) and rotated it for
\texttt{CountDownLatch}. The remaining $170$ generated specs go into the
SFT training pool.

\paragraph{Hand-written reasoning rationales.} The Diamond records have
a four-turn chat structure (developer prompt $\to$ user request $\to$
assistant design rationale $\to$ assistant spec). The rationale turn is
load-bearing: it teaches the student to plan the variable set and the
safety argument before emitting the module. Generating $170$ rationales
by template extraction does not work---a first attempt that
string-formatted rationales from spec metadata produced records with the
same skeleton, which would teach the student to produce that skeleton.
We therefore dispatch the rationale generation as four parallel agents
with strict no-template prompts and a per-batch sanity check that no two
rationales share their first $60$ characters and that every rationale
falls in a $600$--$1500$-character window. The four batches return
$170$ unique-prefix rationales (mean length $1039$, range $813$--$1287$)
that read as per-spec design memos rather than as filled-in templates.

\paragraph{Mixed corpus.} The final training corpus consists of the
existing $713$-record base plus the $170$ new generated specs in
chat-message format oversampled $2\!\times$, for $1053$ records total.
The build script hard-asserts that no record contains any of the
$30$ holdout module names anywhere in its assistant turns.
Oversampling $2\!\times$ is a deliberate compromise: pure incremental
training on only the $170$ new specs risks catastrophic forgetting on
the categories the $713$-record base covers, while training on the
union without oversampling dilutes the gradient contribution of the
new algorithm-family diversity below $20\%$ of the corpus.

\paragraph{Why retry inside the generator instead of outside.} A simpler
design would have a single generator emit many candidates per topic and
filter them downstream, which is closer to the rejection-sampler shape
of \S\ref{sec:diamond-sft}. We do not take that path because the
information the validator returns is rich (tier, distinct-state count,
mutation outcome, specific SANY error string), and feeding it
\emph{back into the generator} is strictly more informative than
treating each candidate as independent. Empirically, agents that get a
\texttt{tier=silver} verdict on attempt $1$ shrink their constants on
attempt $2$; agents that get \texttt{trivial\_invariant=true}
strengthen the safety conjunct on attempt $2$; agents that get a SANY
parse error correct the specific token on attempt $2$. A rejection
sampler that discards failed candidates would have to re-discover the
same fixes from scratch on every retry.

\section{The In-Flight SFT Run and Three Hypotheses}
\label{sec:sftv3}

The mixed $1053$-record corpus of \S\ref{sec:gen} is the input to an
incremental SFT run on top of the deployed merged checkpoint, which is
\texttt{v13}~$+$ checkpoint-131 LoRA already merged. The run uses a
fresh rank-$8$ LoRA adapter, $3$ epochs, learning rate
$5\!\times\!10^{-5}$, max sequence length $4096$, effective batch size
$8$ ($2$ per device $\times$ $2$ GPUs $\times$ $4$ gradient accumulation
steps), bf16, on $2{\times}$RTX 8000 hardware. The total schedule is
$396$ optimisation steps. Tokenisation completed in $2.6$\,s; the model
loaded with all $23$ trainable layers dispatched across the two GPUs by
\texttt{device\_map=auto}; the first optimisation step took $68.5$\,s
and the second took $66.8$\,s, putting the projected wall clock at
roughly $7.4$\,h.

The third optimisation step did not start. The trainer aborted with the
error reproduced in Figure~\ref{fig:checkpoint-error}: the gradient
checkpointing machinery saved a forward-pass tensor of one shape and
recomputed a tensor of an off-by-one shape during the backward pass, on
the second of the two GPUs. Roughly thirty tensor positions are
affected; the offsets are split, some short by one and some long by
one; every affected device is \texttt{cuda:1}.

\paragraph{Why this is non-trivial.} A gradient-checkpoint recomputation
error of this shape can only happen if the forward pass is
non-deterministic in a way that affects the \emph{shape} (not just the
values) of intermediate activations. Three sources of non-determinism
in this stack are plausible.

\begin{description}[leftmargin=1.4em, itemsep=2pt]
\item[\textbf{H1: MoE expert routing.}] \texttt{gpt-oss:20b} is a sparse
  mixture-of-experts model. The router is a top-$k$ softmax over expert
  affinities; if the recomputed forward pass sees a slightly different
  set of selected experts (because of bf16 round-off, dropout, or
  expert-capacity bin-packing), the per-expert sequence length on each
  GPU changes by exactly the difference in token assignments. The
  observed off-by-one shape mismatches on a single GPU are precisely
  what one would expect from a single token being routed to a different
  expert on recompute. This is our leading hypothesis because it
  uniquely explains why \emph{all} affected tensors are on
  \texttt{cuda:1}: GPU-$1$ holds layers $11$--$22$ where most of the
  expert dispatch happens for sequences in this batch.
\item[\textbf{H2: LoRA dropout under reentrant checkpointing.}] The LoRA
  config sets \texttt{lora\_dropout=0.05}. With reentrant gradient
  checkpointing (the default in this version of the trainer), the RNG
  state is not reliably restored on recompute; dropout masks differ
  between the forward pass and the backward pass. This would normally
  produce \emph{value} mismatches, not \emph{shape} mismatches, but in
  combination with downstream MoE routing on the dropped-out activations
  it can cascade into a shape mismatch at the dispatch boundary. This is
  our secondary hypothesis: dropout is the trigger; routing is the
  amplifier.
\item[\textbf{H3: Multi-GPU dispatch and \texttt{device\_map=auto}.}]
  The model is loaded with \texttt{device\_map=auto} and the LoRA
  adapter is restricted to GPU-resident layers $0$--$22$. The boundary
  between \texttt{cuda:0} and \texttt{cuda:1} falls inside the
  transformer stack, and gradient checkpointing across that boundary
  must serialise an activation across PCIe. If the serialised
  activation is sized from a tensor that is itself a router output, the
  recompute can reconstruct it from a slightly different routing
  decision and the boundary tensor is the wrong size. This hypothesis
  predicts that the same training script on a single GPU (with CPU
  offload for the spillover layers) would not fail.
\end{description}

\paragraph{Triage plan.} We are running the three hypotheses as
independent fixes against the same corpus and the same merged
checkpoint. Each fix is a $396$-step training job; the first to clear
$30$ steps without a recompute mismatch becomes the new recipe.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em, itemsep=2pt]
\item Set \texttt{use\_reentrant=False} in the gradient checkpointing
  call (the non-reentrant variant restores RNG state explicitly), and
  set \texttt{lora\_dropout=0.0}. This addresses H2 directly and is the
  cheapest fix to try.
\item Force the MoE router into deterministic mode by pinning
  \texttt{torch.use\_deterministic\_algorithms(True)} and setting the
  CUDA workspace environment variable. This addresses H1 directly but
  may slow training by $2\!\times$.
\item Reload the model with a single-GPU device map and CPU offload for
  layers that do not fit. This addresses H3 by removing the cross-GPU
  boundary entirely; it is the slowest fix but the most diagnostic.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Decision rule.} If any of the three fixes runs to $396$
steps and the resulting checkpoint clears the held-out diamond eval at
$\ge\!8/30$ Diamond pass (against the deployed pre-train baseline of
$3/30$ Diamond, $4/30$ gold), the new adapter is merged through the
existing deploy pipeline and re-tagged as a successor to
\texttt{chattla:20b}. If none of them does, the corpus is the binding
constraint, not the schedule, and the next lever is to widen the
topic curriculum rather than to fight the trainer. The two outcomes
are diagnostic in different directions and we treat them as the
operational definition of progress on this run.

\paragraph{Pre-train baseline on the held-out diamond eval.} Before
launching the SFT, we measured the deployed checkpoint on the
$30$-spec held-out diamond eval with retrieval disabled. The script
prompts the deployed model with each topic description, runs the
generated module through the same Diamond validator used at corpus
build time, and tallies tier counts. The baseline result is $4/30$ at
gold tier of which $3/30$ pass the full Diamond gate---i.e.\ a $10\%$
Diamond pass rate on problems the deployed model has not seen---against
$11/30$ silver and $15/30$ bronze. This is the bar the post-train
checkpoint has to beat.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Why broad SFT keeps failing.} The TLA+ public corpus is tiny
by modern standards and the canonical examples are short, idiomatic, and
structurally similar. A $20$B model already has a passable prior over
this distribution from pre-training, so the marginal value of broad SFT
against more of the same is small. What broad SFT \emph{does} do
reliably is overwrite adjacent parts of the prior---braces, identifiers,
English idioms---in ways that show up as catastrophic forgetting on
benchmark problems where the model would otherwise have stitched
together knowledge from non-TLA+ parts of its training. The one regime
in which we got SFT to help was Diamond-filtered, incremental, low-rate,
short-epoch.

\paragraph{Why preference optimisation against a binary verifier
fails.} A preference loss only has access to the labels you give it. A
TLA+ verifier emits a binary acceptance signal that is satisfied by an
entire equivalence class of specifications, only some of which a human
reviewer would call correct. Optimising hard against the binary signal
moves the model toward the easy-to-reach corners of that class
(degenerate state spaces, decorative invariants), which is exactly the
regime the Diamond audit documents. The fix is to make the reward
semantically sharper before training, not to make the optimiser
stronger. We expect the same conclusion to hold for any
verifier-checked target language with a wide acceptance set: Dafny,
Coq, Lean, Liquid Haskell, SMT pre-conditions.

\paragraph{Why inference-time decomposition is the cheapest win.}
Decomposition costs roughly $5\!\times$ the per-spec inference budget
relative to single-shot, but it requires no training data, no
labelling, no rollback infrastructure, and no risk to the published
checkpoint. In a regime where each round of fine-tuning has roughly
even odds of helping or hurting, ``do not train, generate more
carefully'' is a reasonable default. We do not believe inference-time
methods replace fine-tuning---the Diamond-filtered SFT recipe is what
gives the piecewise procedure something to decompose---but the order
matters. Train incrementally on small, semantically audited corpora;
generate through verifier-guided decomposition; treat broad fine-tuning
as a last resort.

\paragraph{Generalisation across base models.} Every result above uses
one underlying checkpoint, \texttt{gpt-oss:20b}. We do not yet know
whether the catastrophic-forgetting threshold (LR, epoch count, sample
count) generalises to larger or coding-specialised models, or whether
the binary-reward vacuity trap is just as bad with a stronger prior.
The conjecture is that both effects soften with scale but do not vanish:
the first because the prior is more robust to perturbation, the second
because the acceptance-set/target-set gap is a property of the verifier,
not of the model.

\paragraph{Statistical caveats on $N=20$.} The held-out benchmark has
twenty problems. Treating each problem as an independent Bernoulli
trial, a Wilson $95\%$ confidence interval on a point estimate of
$5/20$ is approximately $[0.11, 0.47]$, wide enough that the
\texttt{v13} TLC count and the diamond-SFT TLC count are not
statistically distinguishable; the piecewise count and the early
baselines \emph{are} distinguishable at $\alpha=0.05$ under the same
approximation, but only barely. The right reading of
Table~\ref{tab:run-history} is therefore: the catastrophic deltas
(\texttt{v14} $0/0$, KTO regression to $1/20$ TLC) are large enough
that no plausible CI overlaps the \texttt{v13} peak, and we treat them
as load-bearing signal; the small deltas between adjacent rows
(\texttt{v8} versus \texttt{v9}, \texttt{v13} versus diamond-SFT) sit
inside the wobble and should be read as \emph{directional} only.
Conclusions in \S\ref{sec:lessons} that depend on the catastrophic
deltas are robust to the small $N$; the quick-eval volatility claim is
observed across two hundred cycles and does not depend on benchmark
size.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
\emph{One model.} See above.
\emph{One benchmark.} The held-out suite is small and self-authored.
\emph{No multi-seed.} Every cell in Table~\ref{tab:run-history} is a
single run; informal repeats suggest a $\pm 1$--$2$ problem wobble.
The deltas we treat as load-bearing are larger than the wobble; the
deltas we treat as suggestive are not.
\emph{Curator-bound generator.} The topic curriculum is hand-curated.
Scaling beyond $200$ specs without diminishing returns will likely
require either a larger curated topic list (cheap but bounded by
curator effort) or a generator that proposes new topics by perturbing
existing ones in algorithm-faithful ways.
\emph{Single-style corpus.} Every spec in the generated corpus reflects
one underlying generator's idioms, which the student model will inherit;
whether they generalise to TLA+ written in other styles is not yet
measured.

\paragraph{Related work.} Our negative results echo a long line of work
on catastrophic forgetting in fine-tuning~\cite{kirkpatrick2017ewc,
lopez2017gem}: even moderate-LR SFT on a narrow distribution will
overwrite adjacent capabilities of a base model, and the standard
mitigations---elastic weight consolidation, gradient episodic memory,
replay buffers---all encode the same intuition we eventually adopted by
hand, namely that incremental training on top of a previous merged
checkpoint with a low LR and small epoch budget is safer than
re-deriving the adapter from base. We do not implement these mitigations
explicitly; the augmented archive's best-per-prompt merge plays a similar
role at the data level. For preference optimisation, the literature on
DPO~\cite{rafailov2023direct} and KTO~\cite{ethayarajh2024kto} is largely
silent on the failure mode we encountered, because most reported
applications use human or model-judged preferences with a richer
acceptance set than a binary verifier; the closest published discussion
is the literature on \emph{reward hacking} in RL from verifier feedback.
For fine-tuning on formal-language targets specifically, the closest
prior work we are aware of is on Dafny~\cite{misu2024towards} and on
Isabelle/Coq~\cite{first2023baldur}; both report that broad SFT on small
formal corpora is fragile in ways that mirror our experience, and both
end up using verifier feedback as a selection mechanism rather than as
a gradient.

% ===========================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

A year of fine-tuning a $20$B open model for TLA\textsuperscript{+}
specification generation taught us that the productive interventions
were not the ones we expected. Broad SFT failed in proportion to how
broad it was. Preference optimisation against a binary verifier reward
drifted the model toward semantically vacuous specifications. The
pipeline-engineering work that mattered most was the eval gate and the
rollback path, which prevented a single bad retrain from becoming a
published regression. The single largest improvement on the held-out
benchmark came from a five-step inference-time decomposition that does
not touch the weights at all. The recipe that survived---incremental
SFT on Diamond-filtered, topic-curriculum-generated data, plus piecewise
inference---is small, boring, and reproducible, and that is the point.
For low-resource formal languages with deterministic verifiers, the
leverage is in the shape of the reward and the structure of the
inference, not in the volume of supervised data. The in-flight failure
at the gradient-checkpoint boundary on multi-GPU MoE dispatch is an
interesting reminder that the boring infrastructure layer is, at this
scale, where the next class of failures lives.
